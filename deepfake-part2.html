Part 2 — My Graduation Project

The Dataset — ASVspoof2019 and My Custom Turkish Speech Dataset

Graduation Project EEE 497 · Data Collection & Preparation

Every ML project starts with data. For my deepfake detection system I used two sources: the standard ASVspoof2019 benchmark dataset, and a custom Turkish speech dataset I built myself from scratch. This part explains both — what they contain, why I chose them, and what made collecting my own dataset challenging and rewarding.

📦 Dataset 1 — ASVspoof2019

ASVspoof2019 is the standard benchmark for evaluating automatic speaker verification systems against spoofing. It provides two scenarios — Logical Access (LA) with synthetic speech, and Physical Access (PA) with replay attacks — each with training, development, and evaluation splits and clearly labelled genuine/spoof samples.

This dataset is used by researchers worldwide, which means my results can be directly compared to other published work. Using a standard benchmark was essential for validating that my model actually works — not just on my own data, but on a dataset everyone agrees is representative of the problem.

🎤 Dataset 2 — my custom Turkish speech dataset

ASVspoof2019 is English-only. I wanted to test whether a model trained on English deepfakes could detect Turkish deepfakes — a cross-language generalization challenge that is rarely studied in the literature.

So I built my own dataset. Here are the numbers:

315Total recordings
164Real (genuine)
151Fake (synthetic)
26Speakers

📝 The sentences I recorded

Each participant recorded these three Turkish sentences twice — giving natural variation in speed, intonation, and style:

"Bugün hava oldukça güzel ve yeni projeler üzerinde çalışıyorum."
"Today the weather is quite nice and I am working on new projects."
"Yapay zeka teknolojileri her geçen gün daha hızlı gelişiyor."
"Artificial intelligence technologies are developing faster every day."
"Bu kayıt bir ses işleme araştırması için hazırlanmıştır."
"This recording was prepared for a speech processing research study."

🤖 Generating the fake samples

The same three sentences were also fed into multiple text-to-speech platforms including ElevenLabs and similar services. Using multiple TTS systems ensures diversity in the synthetic samples — the model cannot just learn to detect one specific TTS artifact.

🔨 How I collected the real recordings

1

Recruited 26 participants

Friends, classmates, and family members with different ages, genders, and voice characteristics.

2

Standardized recording setup

Each participant read the same three sentences twice in a quiet environment using their smartphone or laptop microphone.

3

Generated synthetic counterparts

The exact same sentences were synthesized using TTS platforms, creating matched genuine-fake pairs for fair comparison.

4

Labeled and organized

All 315 recordings labeled as genuine (0) or fake (1), organized into folders for model evaluation.

⚠️ Challenges this dataset introduced

Language mismatch — the model was trained on English but tested on Turkish speech patterns
Diverse recording conditions — different microphones, rooms, background noise levels
Multiple TTS systems — different spoofing techniques that the model had never seen
Small dataset size — only 315 samples vs tens of thousands in ASVspoof2019

Collecting the data was actually one of my favorite parts of the project. Asking 26 people to read sentences for me — explaining what I was building and why it mattered — made it feel real. It wasn't just an academic exercise anymore. These were real voices. And I was building something that could protect them.

💡 What I learned about data collection

Building your own dataset is much harder than using an existing one — but far more valuable. It forces you to think about every assumption your model makes: which languages, which recording conditions, which spoofing techniques. My custom dataset revealed limitations in the model that the benchmark never would have shown. That is exactly the point of cross-dataset evaluation.
