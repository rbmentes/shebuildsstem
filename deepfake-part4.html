✅ Experiment 1 — LA baseline (the strong result)

The model trained and tested on the LA dataset achieved 99.27% accuracy and an EER of just 1.41%. This is an excellent result — the confusion matrix shows only 87 false positives and 93 false negatives out of over 24,000 samples. The ROC curve hugs the top-left corner, the training loss decreased smoothly, and the EER kept improving with each epoch down to 1.41% by epoch 4.

This result tells us the model can reliably detect algorithmically generated speech when trained and tested on the same distribution. The LCNN architecture with log-Mel spectrograms is highly effective for this task.

⚠️ Experiment 2 — PA baseline (the harder task)

Replay attacks are harder to detect. The baseline model achieved an EER of 12.77% — significantly higher than LA. This makes sense: a replay attack is real human speech that has been recorded, played back, and re-recorded. The speech content is genuine. Only subtle acoustic distortions from the playback device and room acoustics make it "fake."

After applying data augmentation (adding noise during training), the EER improved to 11.01% — a 13.79% relative improvement. The AUC increased from 0.937 to 0.955. Augmentation helps because it forces the model to look beyond surface noise patterns and find the real acoustic artifacts.

🌍 Experiments 3 & 4 — cross-language generalization

The most interesting and honest results came from testing on my custom Turkish dataset. Training was on English (ASVspoof) and testing on Turkish speech — a true domain mismatch.

Experiment 3 (LA→Turkish): EER of 6.67%. Surprisingly decent — the model trained on English TTS artifacts generalized partially to Turkish TTS artifacts. This suggests that synthetic speech from similar TTS systems leaves detectable patterns regardless of language.

Experiment 4 (PA→Turkish): EER of 18.76% — the worst result. Here the model was trained on replay attack detection but tested on TTS-generated speech. These are fundamentally different spoofing techniques, so the model's learned features simply did not transfer.

This noise sensitivity is a real limitation. In real-world deployment, audio quality varies enormously. Future work should incorporate noise-robust feature extraction and training with diverse noise augmentation.

💡 Four things I learned from this project

Matched vs mismatched conditions are very different

A model that achieves 1.41% EER in a controlled setting can struggle at 18.76% in a real-world cross-language scenario. Benchmark results alone don't tell the whole story.

Attack type matters more than language

LA→Turkish worked reasonably well. PA→Turkish failed. The type of spoofing technique matters more than language for cross-dataset transfer.

Data augmentation genuinely helps

Adding noise during PA training improved EER from 12.77% to 11.01%. Small improvements in EER represent significant real-world impact in security systems.

Building your own dataset teaches you the most

Collecting 315 recordings from 26 speakers and generating synthetic counterparts taught me more about the problem than reading 20 papers.

This project is the thing I'm proudest of from my undergraduate years. Not because the results were perfect — they weren't. But because I built something real, tested it honestly, acknowledged its limitations, and learned exactly why those limitations exist. That's what engineering is.

If I continue this research, I would focus on domain adaptation — techniques that help a model trained in one context perform better in another. The gap between benchmark performance and real-world performance is the most important unsolved problem in deepfake detection.

— Rabia Buse Menteş, EEE Graduate

💡 Series summary

I built an LCNN-based deepfake speech detection system, trained it on ASVspoof2019, built my own Turkish dataset, and ran 4 cross-dataset experiments. The system works excellently under controlled conditions but struggles with domain mismatch — which is the core unsolved problem of the field. The findings are honest, the limitations are clear, and the next steps are specific. That's what a good graduation project looks like.
