What Is Deepfake Audio and Why Does It Matter?

Graduation Project EEE 497 · Adana Alparslan Türkeş Science and Technology University

For my graduation project in Electrical and Electronics Engineering, I built a deepfake speech detection system. This series documents everything — the problem, the data, the model, the results, and what I learned along the way. This first post covers the basics: what deepfake audio actually is and why detecting it is so important.

I chose this topic because it sits at the intersection of two things I care about: signal processing and AI. Deepfake audio is not a hypothetical threat — it is being used for fraud and misinformation right now. Building a system to detect it felt meaningful.

🎭 What is deepfake audio?

Deepfake audio is synthetic speech that sounds like a real person but was generated by an AI system. Using deep learning techniques, these systems can clone voices, generate new speech, or replay existing recordings — often with the intent to deceive.

The rapid advancement of text-to-speech (TTS) and voice conversion (VC) technologies means that today anyone can generate convincing synthetic speech using tools like ElevenLabs, Resemble AI, or open source models. What once required a recording studio and hours of editing now takes seconds.

⚠️ Why is this dangerous?

🎭Identity spoofing — impersonating someone's voice to bypass voice authentication systems
💰Financial fraud — calling bank systems or employees pretending to be someone else
📰Misinformation — fabricating audio of politicians, public figures, or experts saying things they never said
🔐Unauthorized impersonation — using someone's voice without consent in harmful contexts

🔬 How deepfake audio is created

🗣️

Text-to-Speech (TTS)

An AI model converts written text into speech that sounds like a specific person. The model is trained on recordings of that person.

🔄

Voice Conversion (VC)

The speaker's identity in an existing recording is changed to sound like someone else — same content, different voice.

📼

Replay Attacks

A genuine recording is played back through a speaker and re-recorded. Simple but surprisingly effective against some detection systems.

🧠

Neural Vocoders

Deep learning models like WaveNet, HiFi-GAN generate ultra-realistic speech waveforms that are nearly indistinguishable from real recordings.

📋 The ASVspoof benchmark

Researchers have created standardized datasets to evaluate detection systems. The most important is the ASVspoof2019 dataset, which I used in my project. It has two scenarios:

🔵 Logical Access (LA)

Synthetic speech from TTS and voice conversion algorithms. The fake audio is generated by software — there is no physical recording involved.

🟡 Physical Access (PA)

Replay attacks — real speech recorded, played back through a speaker, and re-recorded. The challenge is the acoustic artifacts introduced.

The LA scenario is significantly easier than PA. A deepfake generated by an algorithm leaves distinct spectral artifacts. A replay attack sounds much more like genuine speech — the model needs to detect subtle acoustic distortions introduced by the playback device and room acoustics.

🎯 What my project does

My graduation project builds an LCNN (Light Convolutional Neural Network) based system that takes an audio recording, extracts log-Mel spectrogram features, and classifies it as genuine or fake. I trained and evaluated it on both the ASVspoof2019 LA and PA datasets, and additionally created a custom Turkish speech dataset to test cross-language generalization.

💡 Key takeaway from Part 1

Deepfake audio is not a future problem — it exists now and is being misused. The field of deepfake detection is active and challenging, especially when it comes to generalizing across different languages, recording conditions, and spoofing techniques.
