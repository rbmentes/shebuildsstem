Decision Trees & Random Forests

Spring 2026 · Beginner-friendly · From an engineering student

This week we cover two of the most intuitive and widely used ML algorithms — Decision Trees and Random Forests. Unlike neural networks, these models are easy to visualise and explain, which makes them a perfect stepping stone for understanding how machines make decisions.

🌳 What is a Decision Tree?

A Decision Tree is a model that makes predictions by asking a series of yes/no questions about the input features — like a flowchart. At each step it splits the data based on the feature that best separates the classes.

Imagine trying to decide whether an email is spam or not. A decision tree might ask: "Does it contain the word 'free'?" → "Is the sender unknown?" → "Does it have more than 3 links?" Each answer leads to the next question until a final decision is reached.

✂️ How does it decide where to split?

The tree chooses the split that makes the resulting groups as "pure" as possible — meaning each group contains mostly one class. Two common measures of impurity are:

Gini Impurity

Measures how often a randomly chosen element would be incorrectly labelled. A Gini of 0 means a perfectly pure node — all one class.

Information Entropy

Measures the amount of disorder in a node. Lower entropy = more ordered = better split. Used in ID3 and C4.5 algorithms.

⚠️ The problem — overfitting

A single decision tree tends to overfit — it grows too deep and memorizes the training data rather than learning general rules. A tree with no limit will perfectly classify training data but perform poorly on new data.

🌲🌲🌲 What is a Random Forest?

A Random Forest solves the overfitting problem by building many decision trees and combining their votes. Each tree is trained on a random subset of the data and features — so they all learn slightly different things.

⚖️ Decision Tree vs Random Forest

🌳 Decision Tree

  • Single model — easy to visualise
  • Fast to train and interpret
  • Prone to overfitting
  • Sensitive to small data changes

🌲 Random Forest

  • Ensemble of many trees
  • Much more accurate
  • Resistant to overfitting
  • Harder to interpret directly

💡 Where are they used?

📧Spam detection
🏥Medical diagnosis
💳Fraud detection
🎵Music genre classification
🌍Language identification
📈Stock prediction

💡 What I learned this week

Decision Trees make ML transparent — you can see exactly why a prediction was made. Random Forests take that transparency and trade it for power, combining hundreds of imperfect trees into one reliable model. This is the core idea behind ensemble learning — and it appears everywhere in modern ML.
